A. The Four Pillars of Nature's Nutritional System

Padmashri Dr. Subhash Palekar classified nature's system into four key arrangements:

  • Regulation of nutrients through the Food Cycle.
  • Provision of nutrients to roots via Capillary Action.
  • Water provision through Rain by Cyclone.
  • Soil enrichment by the Activities of Local Earthworms.

(2) Water Cycle ğŸ’§

  • 71% of the Earth is covered with water.

(3) Energy Cycle â˜€ï¸

  • The Sun is the ultimate source of energy.
  • Green leaves of plants capture and store solar energy.
  • Herbivores obtain this energy by eating plants.
  • The energy is released back into the system when the plant decomposes or is burned.

(4) Humus (Organic Matter) ğŸŒ±

  • Humus is vital organic matter where constant biological and chemical processes occur.
  • It's formed from the decomposition of biomass by millions of microbes.
  • The Symbiotic Relationship:
    • Plant roots release sucrose (sugar), which feeds the microbes.
    • In return, these microbes create humus near the roots, making nutrients available to the plant.

ğŸ”¬ Experiment: Proving Nature's Balance

Method: Weigh 10 green leaves and 10 dry leaves. Note the difference. Burn the dry leaves and weigh the remaining ashes.

Summary:

  • The weight difference between green and dry leaves is water (returned to the atmosphere).
  • When burned, fire (solar energy) and smoke (carbon dioxide from air) are released.
  • The remaining ashes are the minerals the plant took from the earth.

Conclusion: All elements taken from nature (air, water, earth, sun) ultimately return to their original state, maintaining a perfect balance.

(1) Characteristics of Local Earthworms

  • They eat soil.
  • They don't migrate; they become dormant in the soil if food is scarce.
  • They are highly resilient, surviving extreme heat (deserts) and cold (Himalayas).

(2) Contribution to Healthy Soil

âœ¨ Feature:

Soil Engineers.

âœ¨ Description:

They create billions of holes, making soil porous. These tunnels are lined with enzymes, preventing collapse. This process drastically improves water seepage, reduces soil erosion, and raises the groundwater level.

âœ¨ Description:
