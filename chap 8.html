Chapter 8 - Natural Resources

A. What are Resources? üåç

  • A resource is anything that can satisfy human needs, which humans have the special knowledge and capacity to utilize.
  • Elements become resources only through their utility and human capability to use them.
  • Resources are a product of the interaction between three key elements: Nature, Man, and Culture.

üç¥ Resource as Food

  • Naturally grown fruits and crops from agriculture.
  • Milk, meat, and their products from domestic animals.
  • Fish and other aquatic animals.
  • Honey prepared by honey-bees.

üè≠ Resource as a Source of Raw Material

  • Forest products (wood, herbs), agro-products (cotton, jute).
  • Animal products like wool, hides, and meat.
  • Mineral ores which are the base for many industries.

‚ö° Resource as Energy

  • Fossil Fuels: Coal, petroleum, and natural gas for industrial and domestic use.
  • Renewable Energy: Solar insolation, wind, sea waves, tides, and waterfalls.

3. On the Basis of Re-availability

  • Renewable Resources: These are inexhaustible and can reproduce themselves over time.
    Examples: Solar energy, wind, birds, animals, bioresources.
  • Non-Renewable Resources: These cannot be reused or reformed in the near future once consumed.
    Examples: Minerals, coal, natural gas, petroleum.

D. Planning and Conservation of Resources üå±

Conservation is crucial because human needs are unlimited while resources are limited. It involves rational usage, conservation, and re-usage.

  • First, gather information on the availability of all resources.
  • Use limited and non-renewable resources scientifically and only when necessary.
  • Attempt to develop resources whose quantity can be increased.
  • Use easily available resources sparingly to save them for the future.
  • Develop alternative options for limited resources through technology.
  • Form and strictly enforce laws for resource conservation.
  • Cultivate public awareness about the rational use of resources.

E. Soil: The Foundation of Life üèûÔ∏è

What is Soil?

  • Soil is the uppermost, thin layer of the Earth's crust where vegetation grows.
  • It's a natural mixture of minerals (from denudation of parent rocks) and biotic elements (humus from decayed vegetation/insects).
  • Climate is a very important factor in soil formation.

Types of Soil in India (ICAR Classification)

The Indian Council of Agricultural Research (ICAR) has classified Indian soils into 8 types.

Soil Type Key Features & Description
1. Alluvial Soil Features: Most widespread (43%). Formed by river deposits. Rich in potash, phosphoric acid, limestone; poor in nitrogen.
Description: Found in Northern plains and river deltas. Very fertile. Good for wheat, paddy, sugarcane, cotton.
2. Red Soil Features: Covers 19% of India. Red due to ferric oxide. Deficient in lime, nitrogen, potash.
Description: Found in peninsular India. Good for millet, cotton, wheat, groundnut.
3. Black Soil (Regur) Features: Covers 15% of India. Formed from Deccan lava. High moisture retention, develops fissures when dry. Rich in iron, lime, potash.
Description: Known as 'Black Cotton Soil'. Found in Maharashtra, Gujarat, MP. Ideal for cotton, linseed, mustard.
4. Laterite Soil Features: From Latin 'later' (brick). Red due to iron oxide. Becomes hard when dry. Less fertile.
Description: Formed in areas with alternating dry & moist climate. Good for tea, coffee, cashew (with fertilizers).
5. Desert Soil Features: Sandy and infertile. High content of dissolved minerals.
Description: Found in arid/semi-arid areas like Rajasthan, Kachchh. Can grow millet, jowar with irrigation.
6. Mountain Soil Features: Thin, underdeveloped layer. Found at high altitudes (2700-3000m).
Description: Found in Himalayan valleys (Assam, UK, HP, Kashmir).
7. Forest Soil Features: Found in coniferous forests (3000-3100m). Surface is black due to humus.
Description: Found in Himalayas, Sahyadri. Good for tea, coffee, spices, wheat, maize.
8. Marshy/Peaty Soil Features: Develops in humid regions from accumulated biotic matter. Rich in minerals but lacks phosphate & potash.
Description: Found in coastal areas of Odisha, WB, TN. Submerged in rainy season. Good for paddy.

F. Soil Erosion and Conservation üõ°Ô∏è

What is Soil Erosion?

  • The transportation of soil particles from one place to another by natural forces like moving air (wind) and water.
  • It removes the fertile upper layer of soil, decreasing agricultural production.

Measures to Prevent Soil Erosion

  • Control Grazing: Manage grazing activity to prevent stripping of vegetation cover.
  • Contour Plantation: Plant crops along contours on sloppy regions to slow water flow.
  • Afforestation: Plant trees, especially in fallow land.
  • Check Dams: Construct small dams across streams to reduce water speed and trap silt.
  • Deep Cultivation: Ploughing deeply to increase water absorption and reduce runoff.

Remedies for Soil Conservation

  • Forest Cover: The roots of trees in forests hold soil particles together.
  • Shelter Belts: Plant rows of trees near desert regions to prevent winds from carrying sand.
  • Flood Control: Divert floodwaters into dry river beds or other channels.
  • Terrace Farming: Create step-like terraces on mountain slopes for cultivation.
  • Horizontal Cultivation: Ploughing horizontally across a slope instead of up and down.
  • Add Humus: Add organic matter to soil that has lost its fertility.
