A. Introduction to Minerals ‚õèÔ∏è

Minerals are the backbone of economic development and have played a huge role in human progress, from the Stone Age to the modern Atomic Age.

A mineral is a naturally occurring matter formed through an inorganic process, which has a certain chemical composition, homogeneous constitution, and a specific atomic structure.

Minerals can be solid (Iron, Gold), liquid (Mercury, Petroleum), or gaseous (Natural Gas).

C. Important Minerals and Their Features

Metallic Minerals

Mineral (Ore) Key Features & Uses Major Producing States in India
Iron Ore Base of modern industry; strong, durable, cheap. Used for everything from pins to ships. Pure iron is called 'Pig Iron'. Types: Hematite, Magnetite, Limonite, Siderite. Karnataka (Maximum), Orissa, Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Goa.
Manganese Crucial for steel industry (makes steel flexible & strong). Used in bleaching powder, insecticides, dry batteries, glass, and leather industries. Odisha, Karnataka, Madhya Pradesh, Goa.
Copper First mineral used by man. Good conductor of electricity, forms alloys (Brass with Zinc, Bell Metal with Tin). Used in electricals, coins, printing, insecticides. Jharkhand, Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan. Also in Sikkim, AP.
Bauxite (Aluminium Ore) Source of Aluminium. Very light, strong, durable, rust-free, good conductor. Used in domestic utensils, electrical gadgets, shipbuilding, and colours. Odisha, Chhattisgarh, Maharashtra, Jharkhand, Gujarat.

Non-Metallic Minerals

Mineral (Ore) Key Features & Uses Major Producing States in India
Mica India is the #1 producer in the world. Fire resistant and a bad conductor of electricity. Used in electrical goods like motors, dynamos, radios, telephones. Muscovite is a major type. Bihar, Jharkhand, Andhra Pradesh, Rajasthan.
Lead (Galena) Soft and heavy. Used to make alloys, electric wires, colours, weapons, glass, and storage batteries. Rajasthan, Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu.
Limestone Used in large quantities for cement production. Also used in iron melting, chemical industry, soap, paper, and sugar refining. Andhra Pradesh, Rajasthan, MP, Gujarat, Tamil Nadu (together ~70%).

D. Energy Resources ‚ö°

Classification of Energy Resources

Energy resources are vital for industries and the economy. They are broadly classified as:

  • Conventional / Commercial: These are non-renewable. Examples include Coal, Mineral Oil, Natural Gas, and Atomic Minerals.
  • Non-Conventional / Renewable: These are renewable and generally cleaner. Examples include Solar, Wind, Hydro, Bio-gas, Geothermal, and Tidal energy.

Conventional Energy Resources

Resource Description & Key Points Major Reserves / Production Areas
Coal ‚ö´ Formed from buried vegetation during the Carboniferous period. Main source for thermal electricity. Types by carbon content: (1) Anthracite (best), (2) Bituminous, (3) Lignite, (4) Peat (worst). Jharkhand, Orissa, West Bengal, Chhattisgarh. Lignite in Gujarat (Kachchh, Bharuch).
Mineral Oil ‚õΩ Formed from buried animals/vegetation (hydrocarbons). First well dug in Assam (1866). Refined to get petrol, diesel etc. Oil Zones: North-East, Gujarat (Lunej, Ankleshwar), Bombay High, Eastern Coast, Rajasthan. World's largest refinery is at Jamnagar, Gujarat.
Natural Gas üí® Found with mineral oil. Cheaper and a non-polluting source of energy. Khambhat basin, Kaveri basin, Jaisalmer (Rajasthan). Ankleshwar (Gujarat) has the largest reserve.

Non-Conventional Energy Resources

Resource Description & Key Points Major Projects / Areas in India
Solar Energy ‚òÄÔ∏è Energy from the sun. Used in solar cookers, heaters, and panels for electricity. GEDA (Gujarat Energy Development Agency) is a key body. Gujarat gets maximum solar energy. Projects: Solar cold storage near Chhani (Vadodara), desalination plant at Madhopur (Bhuj).
Wind Energy üçÉ Generated using windmills in coastal and open areas. India is the 5th largest producer. Gujarat, Tamil Nadu, Maharashtra, Andhra Pradesh. Wind farms in Gujarat at Lambha (Jamnagar) and Mandavi (Kachchh).
Bio-Gas üêÑ Methane gas produced from decomposition of agricultural waste, dung, etc. Provides energy and fertilizer. Uttar Pradesh (1st), Gujarat (2nd). Largest plant at Maithan (Siddhpur, Gujarat).
Geothermal Energy üî• Thermal energy from the earth's interior (hot springs, geysers). Steam is used to generate energy. Potential sites in Gujarat: Lasundra, Unai, Tuva, Tulsishyam.
Tidal Energy üåä Energy generated from the gravitational pull of the sun and moon causing tides. Turbines are used to harness this power. India has a long coastline with great potential. Projects started in the Gulf of Kachchh and Gulf of Khambhat.

E. Mineral Preservation ‚ôªÔ∏è

Minerals are finite and non-renewable, so their planned and efficient use (preservation) is necessary for sustainable development.

Remedies for Mineral Preservation

üí° Mnemonic to Remember Remedies: "Tomorrow's Resources Always Need Smart Planning"

  • T - Technology: Use proper technology to reduce wastage during mining and extraction.
  • R - Recycling: Reuse scrap minerals like iron, copper, aluminium, and tin.
  • A - Alternates: Find and use alternative options for scarce minerals (e.g., solar energy for electricity, aluminium for copper).
  • N - Non-conventional: Increase the use of renewable sources like solar, wind, and bio-gas.
  • S - Sustainability: Maintain a pollution-free environment for future generations.
  • P - Planning: After calculating reserves, plan their use for a longer period.
