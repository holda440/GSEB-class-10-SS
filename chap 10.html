Chapter 10 - India: Agriculture 🌱

A. Introduction to Indian Agriculture

Importance & Status

  • Agriculture is a prime economic activity and the main support for the Indian economy.
  • Workforce: About 60% of India's labour power is engaged in agriculture.
  • National Product: Occupies about 22% of the national product.
  • Exports: Farm products contribute about 18% to exports, earning foreign exchange.
  • Identity: India is primarily an agrarian country.

Challenges in Indian Agriculture 😥

  • Farmer Condition: Generally poor and uneducated.
  • Low Production: Output is less compared to the world average.
  • Key Reasons:
    • Inadequate irrigation facilities.
    • Irregular and uncertain rain.
    • High population pressure & large families leading to small, fragmented farms.
    • Lack of interest in experimentation and modern methods.
    • Low usage of chemical fertilizers and modern machinery.
    • Educated class moving away from agriculture.

B. Types of Farming

(1) Subsistence Farming

  • Feature: Farming for self-consumption.
  • Description: Production is just enough to maintain the farmer's family. Caused by weak economic conditions, small farm holdings, and the inability to afford costly seeds, fertilizers, and insecticides.

(2) Dry Farming

  • Feature: Rain-dependent farming in areas with inadequate rainfall.
  • Description: A single crop is grown using humidity accumulated in the land. Crops grown require less water.
  • Crops: Jowar, millet, pulses. In Gujarat, wheat and gram are grown in humid soils post-monsoon.

(3) Wet Farming

  • Feature: Irrigation-based farming in regions of heavy rain or with adequate irrigation.
  • Description: More than one crop can be grown annually using irrigation if rainfall is insufficient.
  • Crops: Paddy, sugarcane, cotton, wheat, vegetables.

(4) Shifting (Jhoom) Agriculture

  • Feature: "Slash and burn" agriculture.
  • Description: Forests are cleared and burnt for farming. The land is used for 2-3 years until soil fertility reduces, then abandoned for a new plot. Production is low.
  • Crops: Cereals or vegetables.

(5) Plantation Agriculture

  • Feature: Large-scale farming of a single crop for commercial purposes.
  • Description: Requires high capital investment, technical knowledge, machinery, fertilizers, and good transportation facilities. Crops are reared with great care.
  • Crops: Rubber, tea, coffee, cocoa, coconut, apple, mango, grapes.

(6) Intensive Farming (Commercial Farming)

  • Feature: Mechanized farming with a focus on high yield and economic returns.
  • Description: Utilizes increased irrigation, chemical fertilizers, insecticides, and mechanical equipment. Per-hectare production is very high.
  • Crops: Cash crops are grown more in this type.

C. Modern Farming Methods

(1) Organic Farming 🌿

  • Feature: Farming without chemical fertilizers and pesticides.
  • Description: Uses natural inputs like dung, earthworm fertilizer, cow urine, and neem solvents. Products are nutritious, natural, and in high demand, offering good returns.

(2) Sustainable Farming

  • Feature: Farming that maintains long-term soil fertility.
  • Description: Focuses on crop rotation, judicious use of fertilizers, biotic controls for pests, and water conservation.

(3) Mixed Farming

  • Feature: Combining crop cultivation with livestock rearing.
  • Description: Activities like cattle rearing, poultry, sericulture (silk farming), and fishery are carried out alongside farming to supplement income.

D. Agricultural Products of India (Crop Seasons)

Kharif (Monsoon) Crops 🌧️

  • Sowing: June-July
  • Harvest: Oct-Nov
  • Crops: Paddy, Maize, Jowar, Millet, Cotton, Til, Groundnut

Rabi (Winter) Crops ❄️

  • Sowing: Oct-Nov
  • Harvest: March-April
  • Crops: Wheat, Gram, Barley, Mustard, Linseed

Zaid (Summer) Crops ☀️

  • Sowing: March
  • Harvest: June
  • Crops: Paddy, Maize, Groundnut, Watermelon, Cucumber, Musk Melon

🧠 Mnemonics to Remember Crops

  • Kharif (Monsoon): "Kharif needs Rain". Imagine eating **Rice (Paddy)** and **Maize** roti, wearing **Cotton** clothes during a bad rainy day.
  • Rabi (Winter): "Rabi in a Rajaai (Quilt)". Imagine eating warm **Wheat** rotis with **Mustard** greens (Sarson ka Saag) wrapped in a quilt.
  • Zaid (Summer): Think of juicy, watery summer fruits! **Watermelon**, **Cucumber**, etc.

E. Major Crops of India 🌾

Food Grains (Khadya Pak)

  • Paddy (Rice): India is the 2nd largest producer after China. A crop of the torrid zone needing a hot, humid climate, >100 cm rainfall. Major states: WB, TN, AP, Punjab, Haryana.
  • Wheat: 2nd most important crop. A rabi crop needing fertile loamy soil, >75 cm rainfall. Punjab is called the 'Wheat Bowl of India'. It is the "king of grains".
  • Millets (Jowar, Bajra): Coarse grains. Jowar is grown in dry regions. Bajra (Millet) is the grain of labourers, grown in sandy soil.
  • Maize: A kharif crop, used as food, animal fodder, and for industrial products like starch and bio-fuel.
  • Pulses (Tuver, Mung, Gram): Main source of protein. Grown in all states except high rainfall areas. They fix nitrogen in the soil.

Oil Seeds (Telibiya)

  • Groundnut: Kharif crop. India is the 2nd largest producer. Gujarat leads in production in India.
  • Sesam (Til): Used for oil since ancient times. India is a major exporter.
  • Mustard: A rabi crop, important in North India.
  • Castor Oil (Divela): Both kharif and rabi crop. India is the largest producer in the world (64% share).

Beverages (Pina)

  • Tea: Tropical/temperate plant. India is the 2nd largest producer. Requires sloppy land, 200 cm rainfall. Major states: Assam, West Bengal (produce 75% of India's tea).
  • Coffee: Grown on mountain slopes in shade. Requires 150-200 cm rainfall. Major states: Karnataka, Kerala, Tamil Nadu. Coorg in Karnataka is famous for coffee.
  • Cocoa: Used to make beverages and chocolate. Requires hot, humid climate.

Cash Crops (Rokadiya Pak)

  • Cotton: Kharif crop. Known as 'White Gold'. India is 2nd in the world. Requires black lava soil, hot climate. Gujarat is first in India in production due to BT cotton.
  • Sugarcane: India ranks 2nd after Brazil. Used for sugar, gur, khandsari, ethanol. UP has the largest area, but Maharashtra leads in production.
  • Jute: Known as 'Golden Fibre'. India is the largest producer. Requires fertile delta soil. Majorly grown in the Ganga delta (West Bengal).
  • Tobacco: Kharif crop. India is a major producer and exporter. Charotar region in Gujarat is famous. Sikkim is the first state to ban tobacco-gutkha.
  • Rubber: Made from latex of trees. Requires hot, humid climate. Malaysia is 1st in the world. In India, Kerala is the leading producer.

F. Agricultural Reforms in India 🚜

Technical Reforms

  • Irrigation: Shift from leather buckets to submersible pumps, solar pumps, drip irrigation, and sprinklers.
  • Inputs: Use of improved seeds (hybrid, BT seeds), chemical fertilizers (DAP, NPK), and bio-fertilizers.
  • Information Dissemination: Guidance through Radio, TV (DD Kisan), newspapers, Kisan Call Centre (1800 180 1551), and mobile apps (i-khedut).
  • Support: Farmer training centers and Agricultural Universities (e.g., Dantiwada, Junagadh in Gujarat).

Institutional Reforms

  • Land Reforms: Abolition of landlordism (Zamindari system) and implementation of the 'Land to the Tiller' and Land Ceiling Acts.
  • Financial Support: Crop subsidy, Kisan Credit Card, and loans from nationalised banks.
  • Insurance: Prime Minister Crop Insurance Scheme (Pradhan Mantri Fasal Bima Yojana).
  • Market Support: Legal support for open auctions in marketing yards (APMC).
  • Organizations: NAFED, GROFED, and NDDB assist farmers in getting support prices.

G. Green Revolution & Economic Impact

Green Revolution (1960s)

  • Definition: An extraordinary increase in agricultural production due to improved seeds, chemical fertilizers, farmer efforts, and better irrigation.
  • Objective: To increase agricultural production and achieve self-sufficiency in food grains.
  • Impact:
    • Record production of wheat and paddy.
    • Created a buffer stock of food grains, mitigating the effects of drought.
    • Made India self-sufficient in food grains, a landmark achievement.

Role of Agriculture in the Indian Economy 💰

  • Employment: Provides employment to about half of the population.
  • GDP Contribution: Contributes about 17% to the Gross Domestic Product (GDP).
  • Industrial Raw Material: Supplies raw materials to industries like cotton textile, sugar, paper, and food processing.
  • Food Security: Ensures food supply for the nation's vast population. India's food grain production reached a record 265.04 crore tonnes in 2013-14.

Impact of Globalization

  • Positive: Indian farmers can sell products in world markets. Export/import process simplified. New varieties (e.g., BT seeds) available.
  • Negative: Farming has become costly. Domestic products face tough competition from imports.
  • Way Forward: India needs to adopt new technology, focus on quality, and register patents for unique products to compete globally. A "second green revolution" is needed.
