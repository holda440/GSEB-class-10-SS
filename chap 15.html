A. Understanding Economic Development üìà

Meaning of Economic Development

  • Economic Development is a multi-faceted process of social change, not just about money. It has three main goals:
    • A constant increase in the **National Income** of a country.
    • An increase in the **Per Capita Income** of a country.
    • An overall **Improvement in the Living Standard** of the people.
  • Key Terms:
    • National Income: The total income of a country (also referred to as GDP).
    • Per Capita Income: Calculated by dividing the National Income by the Total Population. It represents the average income per person.
    • Living Standard: Includes access to essential facilities like food, clothing, shelter, education, health services, and transportation.

C. Structure of an Economy üèóÔ∏èüè≠üßë‚Äçüíª

Economic vs. Non-Economic Activities

  • Economic Activities: Actions involving earning or spending money for goods/services.
    • Examples: A farmer farming, a teacher teaching, a businessman trading.
  • Non-Economic Activities: Actions not aimed at obtaining income.
    • Examples: A mother nurturing her child, a social worker providing free services.

The Three Sectors of the Economy

The Building Blocks of an Economy

(1) Primary Sector (Agriculture & Related)

Extracts raw materials from nature. Includes agriculture, cattle rearing, fishing, mining, and forestry.

(2) Secondary Sector (Industry)

Transforms raw materials into finished goods. Includes small to large scale industries, manufacturing, construction, electricity, and gas supply.

(3) Service Sector (Tertiary)

Provides services rather than goods. Includes trade, communication, transport, education, health, banking, insurance, and tourism.

In developing countries, the economy typically shifts from being dominated by the Primary sector towards the Secondary and Service sectors as development occurs.

D. The Economic Engine: Factors & Allocation ‚öôÔ∏è

Factors of Production

These are the essential inputs used to produce goods and services.

üß† Remember with MNEMONIC: C.E.L.L.

Capital | Entrepreneurship | Land | Labour

  • Land: Not just soil, but all natural resources (rivers, forests, minerals). It's a natural tool for production.
  • Labour: Mental or physical work done to earn money. It is the living factor of production (e.g., workers, doctors, teachers).
  • Capital: Man-made instruments used in production (e.g., tools, machinery, buildings, money/investment).
  • Entrepreneurship: The skill of combining Land, Labour, and Capital effectively to make a profit. The person who does this is an entrepreneur.

The Problem of Allocation: Why we must choose

Every country faces the challenge of allocating its scarce resources because of a fundamental economic problem:

  • Unlimited Wants: Human wants are endless. Fulfilling one want often leads to another.
  • Limited Resources: The factors of production (land, labour, capital) are finite.
  • Alternative Uses: Resources can be used in multiple ways (e.g., a piece of land can be used for farming wheat OR maize, but not both).

This forces individuals and nations to prioritize their wants and decide how to use limited resources to satisfy the most important ones first.

E. How are Resources Managed? Economic Systems üó∫Ô∏è

Countries adopt different systems to allocate their resources. The main types are Market, Socialist, and Mixed systems.

(A) Market System (Capitalism / "Free Economy")

  • Motive: Profit is the central driving force.
  • Ownership: Resources are privately owned.
  • Control: No government interference. The market is run by the 'invisible hand' of competition and price mechanism.
  • Pros: Economic freedom, efficiency, innovation, high production, better quality.
  • Cons: Neglects basic needs for luxury goods, resource wastage, consumer exploitation, income inequality, risk of monopoly.
  • Examples: USA, Japan.

(B) Socialist System

  • Motive: Social welfare is the central goal.
  • Ownership: All resources are owned and managed by the State.
  • Control: All economic decisions (what to produce, how much, for whom) are made by the State.
  • Pros: Production for societal needs, no resource wastage, income equality, consumers are not exploited.
  • Cons: Lack of motivation and encouragement for individuals, no competition leading to stagnation, lack of individual freedom, fear of bureaucracy.
  • Examples: Russia and China (historically).

(C) Mixed Economy ("Controlled Economic System")

  • Motive: A blend of profit and social welfare.
  • Ownership: Co-existence of public (State) and private sectors. They work as complements, not competitors.
    • Private Sector: Agriculture, trade, small consumer industries.
    • Public Sector: Heavy industries, defence, railways, electricity.
  • Control: The market is not completely free. The government uses controls like taxes, subsidies, and economic planning to guide the economy.
  • Pros: Aims to include the benefits of both systems while removing their limitations.
  • Cons: Can suffer from economic instability, lack of coordination, inconsistent policies, and slow growth.
  • Examples: India, France, England.

  • Economic Progress: Quantitative increase, associated with developed nations.
  • Developing Economy (2004 World Bank): Per capita income < $735.

  • Primary Sector: Based on natural resources (e.g., farming, mining).
  • Secondary Sector: Based on manufacturing (industry).
  • Service Sector: Based on providing services (e.g., banking, tourism).
  • Factors of Production: Land, Labour, Capital, Entrepreneurship.

  • Socialist System's main motive: Social Welfare.
  • Mixed Economy is also called: Controlled Economic System.
