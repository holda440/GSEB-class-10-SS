What is Communalism?

  • Religion is a personal matter of faith, but communal behaviour is against the spirit of India's secular constitution.
  • Communalism arises when a group of one religion/sect opposes another, promoting their own religion as superior.
  • It leads to viewing citizens through a communal lens rather than as individuals, which divides society.

Why is Communalism Harmful?

  • Social Tension: It creates an atmosphere of hatred and difference, turning friends into enemies.
  • Violence: It leads to communal riots and quarrels.
  • Hindrance to Progress: It is harmful to democracy, national integrity, and the development of the nation.

  • Government Action: Take punitive action against communal elements.
  • Education: Promote respect for all religions through curriculum, prayers, and social functions.
  • Television & Media: Use Radio, TV, and Cinema to spread messages of secularism and tolerance.
  • Political Parties: Do not recognize parties based on communal ideas; enforce special ethics for elections.

  • Develop (Youth Attitude): Encourage youth to develop secular and scientific attitudes.
  • Effort (Society): The entire society, not just the government, must work towards eradication.

The Roots of Casteism

  • India's social structure has been based on casteism, which was originally a task-based division of work (Varna system: Brahman, Kshatriya, Vaisya, Shudra).
  • Occupation determined income, leading to economic disparity among castes.
  • Historically, some communities lived in isolation (forests, mountains) and remained socially and economically backward.

Constitutional Provisions to Protect Weaker Sections

  • Goal: To eradicate social inequality, protect interests, and ensure welfare and development for minorities and backward classes.
  • Equal Justice: Grants equal social, economic, and political justice to all Indians.
  • No Discrimination: Prohibits discrimination based on community, caste, religion, language, sex, etc.
  • Equal Opportunity: Guarantees equal opportunity and status as a fundamental right.
  • Welfare State Powers: Allows the state to impose restrictions on fundamental rights to protect weaker sections.
  • Freedom of Religion: Guarantees the right to follow any religion.

C. Minorities, Scheduled Castes (SC) & Scheduled Tribes (ST) üõ°Ô∏è

Understanding Minorities

  • A 'minority' is a group forming less than half the population in a specific region, based on religion or language. This can differ at national vs. state levels.
  • Minorities have equal rights and special provisions to protect their culture and interests.
Feature/Right Description
National Minority Commission Set up to protect the rights, interests, and welfare of minorities.
Right to Religion Minorities can propagate their religion (forceful conversion is illegal). Religious institutions can acquire and maintain property.
Cultural & Educational Rights Right to preserve script and culture. Can establish and run their own educational institutions. No government-aided institution can deny admission based on religion, race, etc.
Primary Education State governments provide facilities for primary education in the mother tongue for minority children.

Scheduled Castes (SC) & Scheduled Tribes (ST)

  • Scheduled Castes (Article 341): Castes included in a special schedule by order of the President. The provisions aim to end exploitation and ensure their progress.
  • Scheduled Tribes (Article 342): People, often living in forests and mountainous areas, with distinct cultures, who are economically and educationally backward.

Key Constitutional Articles for SC/ST

General Provisions (For All Weaker Sections)

  • Article 15: Prohibits discrimination and ensures access to public places (shops, hotels, wells, roads).
  • Article 29: Right to preserve language, script, and culture; no denial of admission to government-aided institutions.

Special Provisions (Targeted)

  • Article 16(4): Allows reservation in government jobs if a class is not adequately represented.
  • Article 46: State must promote educational and economic interests of weaker sections, especially SC/ST, and protect them from social injustice.
  • Articles 330, 332, 334: Reservation of seats for SC/ST in Lok Sabha and State Legislative Assemblies (not in Rajya Sabha).

Provisions ONLY for Scheduled Castes

  • Article 17: üö´ Untouchability is totally eradicated and is a punishable crime.
  • Article 25: Allows states to make laws opening Hindu religious institutions to all sections of Hindus (includes Sikhs, Jains, Buddhists).

Provisions ONLY for Scheduled Tribes

  • Article 19(5): Empowers governors to impose restrictions on movement and property acquisition in tribal areas to prevent their exploitation.

Insurgency and Terrorism in India

  • Naxalist Movement: Inspired by Mao-Tse-tung, started in Naxalbari (West Bengal) in 1967. Spread to Jharkhand, Bihar, Andhra Pradesh, etc. Major groups: People's War Group, Maoist Communist Centre.
  • Insurgency in North-East: A perennial problem due to many tribal communities, hilly terrain, and foreign interference. Groups like NSCN (Nagaland), ULFA (Assam), etc., demand separate states or autonomy.
  • Terrorism in Kashmir: Increased after 1988, supported from across the border. Involves kidnapping, murders, and bombings, leading to the migration of thousands of Kashmiri Pandits. The aim is to create fear and instability.
