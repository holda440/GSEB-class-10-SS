Chapter 13 - Manufacturing Industries

A. INTRODUCTION & IMPORTANCE üè≠

What is an Industry?

  • The process where natural resources are transformed into usable goods using human intellect, cultural, and economic capacity.

Importance of Industries

  • The backbone of a nation's economic development and prosperity.
  • Industrially developed nations (USA, Russia, Japan) have strong economies.
  • They enable a country to harness its natural resources effectively, adding value instead of selling them cheap.
  • In India, manufacturing industries contribute about 29% to the national production.

B. HISTORY OF INDIAN INDUSTRY üìú

Ancient & Pre-British Era

  • Indus Valley Civilization: Traditions of making cotton textiles, clay utensils, bronze items, and beads.
  • Up to 18th Century: India was a leader in shipbuilding, handicrafts, and cottage industries. High global demand for Indian cotton, muslin, and metalwork.

Impact of British Rule

  • The Britishers' policy of sourcing raw materials from India and selling finished goods back at high prices led to the collapse of the Indian market.
  • This severely damaged the indigenous textile industry and the livelihood of Indian craftsmen.

Key Milestones in Modern Industry

  • 1853: First (failed) attempt at an iron furnace.
  • 1854: First successful cotton textile mill established. üöÄ
  • 1855: First jute mill established at Risra (near Kolkata).
  • 1907: A new era began with the founding of the Tata Iron and Steel Company (TISCO) at Jamshedpur.

D. AGRO-BASED INDUSTRIES üåæ

Cotton Textile Industry üëï

Feature: Largest employer in India, providing jobs to about 3.5 crore people.
Description: India ranks second in the world (after China) in the export of cotton textiles. It is a key industry for both production and employment.
  • Major Centres
    • Mumbai: Known as the "Cottonopolis of India".
    • Ahmedabad: Called the "Manchester of the East" or "Denim City of India".
    • Other centres include Coimbatore, Kanpur, Kolkata, Indore, and Ujjain.
  • Challenges Faced
    • Inadequate supply of high-quality cotton.
    • Use of old and outdated machinery.
    • Irregular electricity supply.
    • Stiff competition from synthetic fibres and global markets.

Jute Textile Industry üëú

Feature: India is the largest producer of jute and second-largest exporter (after Bangladesh).
Description: West Bengal is the dominant producer, accounting for about 80% of the country's total jute production. Most mills are located on the banks of the Hooghly river due to the need for water, cheap labour, and port facilities.

Silk Textile Industry üß£

Feature: India is the second-largest producer of silk in the world (after China).
Description: Produces four unique types of silk. Faces tough competition from China in the international market.

Other Agro-Based Industries

  • Woollen Textile Industry: Centered in Punjab, Maharashtra, and Rajasthan. Exports woollen cloth and carpets.
  • Sugar Industry: Second largest agrarian industry. Factories located near sugarcane fields as cane must be processed within 24 hours. Major states are Maharashtra and UP.
  • Paper Industry: Uses softwood, bamboo, and grass. Spread across states like Andhra Pradesh, Maharashtra, and West Bengal.

E. MINERAL-BASED INDUSTRIES ‚õèÔ∏è

Iron and Steel Industry üî©

Feature: Considered the "axis" or "key industry" of modern economic development.
Description: Forms the base for manufacturing machines and infrastructure. India ranks 5th in the world in iron and steel production. Most major plants are managed by the Steel Authority of India Limited (SAIL).

Major Steel Plants:

Jamshedpur (TISCO), Kulti, Durgapur, Bhilai, Rourkela, Bokaro, Salem, and a mini plant in Hajira (Gujarat).

Aluminium Refining Industry ‚úàÔ∏è

Feature: Highly electricity-intensive, consuming 40-50% of production costs.
Description: Bauxite is the primary ore. The industry thrives where bauxite and cheap hydro-electricity are available. Used in cars, railways, and aircraft. Located in states like Odisha and West Bengal.

Other Key Mineral-Based Industries

  • Copper Refining: Essential for the electrical industry. Domestic production is insufficient, leading to imports.
  • Chemical Industry: Gujarat is a leading state. Includes organic (petrochemicals) and inorganic chemicals.
  • Cement Industry: India is the 2nd largest producer. Factories are located near raw material sources (limestone, coal) due to weight.
  • Plastic Industry: Known as the 'Sunrise Industry' due to its growing demand and versatility.

F. TRANSPORT & ELECTRONIC INDUSTRIES üöÜüì±

Transport Equipment Industry

  • Railways: Produces its own engines (diesel, electric) and coaches. Heritage steam engines run for tourism.
  • Road Vehicles: India is 5th in commercial vehicle production. Exports vehicles and spare parts.
  • Ship Building: Major public sector shipyards at Vishakhapatnam, Kochi, Mumbai.
  • Air Craft: Helicopter production units exist, but passenger aircraft manufacturing has not yet started.

Electronic Industry

Feature: Bengaluru is the capital, known as the "Silicon Valley of India".
Description: Started in 1905 with radio/telephone manufacturing. Bharat Electronics Limited (BEL) was a major milestone. The industry's future is considered very optimistic.

G. INDUSTRIAL POLLUTION & REMEDIES üåç

Environmental Degradation

  • Industries cause four main types of pollution: air (harmful gases), water (effluents), land, and noise.
  • This degradation reduces the quality of the environment.

Remedies to Curb Pollution

üí° Key Strategies for a Greener Industry üí°

PLAN industrial development properly.
SELECT high-quality equipment and cleaner fuels.
FILTER air pollutants using scrubbers and precipitators.
TREAT industrial wastewater before releasing it into rivers.
