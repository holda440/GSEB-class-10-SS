A. Introduction: Language and Literature

From ancient times, Indian literature is known for its diversity and uniqueness.

Indian scholars divided ancient literature into Vedic and Classical literature. Folk literature was also popular.

Variety of languages emerged over centuries, influencing each other and leading to literature development.

Sanskrit is a prime example: less in current use but vital for religious ceremonies.

Language facilitates better human expression and understanding.

India's most ancient script is of Harappan civilization (undeciphered).

Sanskrit Language üìú

Grammarian: Maharshi Panini was a great Sanskrit grammarian.
Key Work: Panini's 'Ashtadhyayi' (4th century) is a noteworthy Sanskrit grammar book.
Significance: Considered language of 'Aryans', sages, scholars. Internationally accepted as best language for computer use.
Chief Language For: Knowledge, science, religion, and philosophy.

B. Ancient Indian Literature

Vedas (Veda means 'Knowledge') üôè

There are four Vedas. They are the cornerstone of ancient Indian wisdom.

Remember the 4 Vedas (R-Y-S-A):
"**R**eal **Y**ogis **S**ing **A**lways"
(Rigveda, Yajurveda, Samveda, Atharvaveda)

(1) Rigveda

Antiquity: Most ancient work/book of Indian literature.
Structure: Consists of 1028 verses, divided into 10 divisions (mandalas).
Content: Most verses are prayers to Gods, used during yagnas. Verses worshipping Usha (Goddess of dawn) are fascinating.
Scope: Describes political, social, and religious matters of Aryans in the Saptsindhu region.

(2) Samveda üé∂

Focus: Composed after Rigveda, focuses on the process of recitation of verses.
Nickname: Known as the Gangotri of music.

(3) Yajurveda

Nature: Called a Veda of Yagnas (sacrificial rites).
Form: Composed in both prose and verse.
Content: Describes hymns recited during Yagyas, religious practices, and rituals.

(4) Atharvaveda

Content: Describes various types of rituals and sanskaras (sacraments/rites of passage).

Upanishads üí≠

Focus: Vivid description and analysis of the beginning of the universe, mysteries of life and death, materialistic and spiritual world.
Earliest Upanishads: 'Brihdaranya' and 'Chhandogya' (in dialogue form).
Total Number: 108, as mentioned in Muktiko Upanishad.

Brahmanical Literature

Composed to understand Vedic literature, often in poetic form.

Includes critical appreciation of Vedas and explanations of directive principles regarding behavior.

Aranyakas (Forest Books) üå≥

Origin: Composed by Aryans spending their last phase of life in aranyakas (forests/ashrams).
Content: Based on philosophy, result of deep thinking.

Vedangas (Limbs of Vedas)

Branches: Six branches including religious practices, rituals, grammar, astrology, and astronomy.

Epics: Ramayana & Mahabharata ‚öîÔ∏è

Two great Indian epics, present form traced to 2nd century CE.

Ramayana

Story: Ramchandra, king of Ayodhya.
Size: Smaller epic than Mahabharata, narrates attractive adventure stories.

Mahabharata

Size: World's largest epic, one lakh verses.
Content: War between Kauravas and Pandavas, numerous stories.
Bhagwad Gita: Part of Mahabharata, expounds deep philosophical principles. Conveys message of 'Moksha' (salvation) through 'Gyana' (knowledge), 'Karma' (action), and 'Bhakti' (devotion).

Both epics have influenced millions and implanted motivational seeds of sanskars.

Puranas & Smrutis

Sanskrit literature produced during this period combined religious and worldly aspects.

Puranas: Played an important role in explaining initial Vedic religion of earlier times.

Smruthi Granthas: Dealt with science, philosophy (e.g., Kautilya's 'Arthshastra' - book on management, arts, mathematics, sciences), religious teachings, laws, and customs.

Buddhist Literature ‚ò∏Ô∏è

Language: Earlier Buddhist literature written in Pali.
Key Compilation: 'Tripitika' (Three Baskets/Sections): Sukta Pitika, Vinaya Pitika, Abhidhamma Pitika.

Many other works composed in Buddhist literature.

Gupta Period Literature (Golden Period for Sanskrit Poems & Dramas) ‚ú®

Great writers like Kalidas, Bhavbhuti, Bharvi, Bhartrihari, Banabhatt, Mangha.

Kalidas (Known worldwide)

Works: 'Kumarsambhava', 'Raghuvansham', 'Meghdoot', 'Abhigyan Shankuntalam', 'Rutusamhar' (renowned for poetic and dramatic style).

Other Notable Works & Authors:

Banabhatt: 'Kadambari', 'Harshacharit' (biography of King Harsha).
Bhavbhuti: 'Uttar Ramcharit'.
Bharavi: 'Kiratarjuniyam'.
Vishakhadatta: 'Mudra Raksha'.
Shudrak: 'Mrichchhkatikam'.
Dandi: 'Dashkumarcharita'.

Subjects: Political events, romance, allegories, comedies, philosophy.

Early Gujarati Literature üé∂

Verses composed in earlier form, language developed over time.

Prospered by contributions of: Narsinh Mehta, Mirabai, Dayaram, Akho, Premchand, Pritam (verses, songs, garba, narrative poems, chhappas).

Later scholars: Narmad, Navalram, Kishorlal Mashruwala, Pannalal Patel, Umashankar Joshi, Mahipatram Ruparam Nilkanth, Goverdhanram Tripathi.

C. Dravidian Languages Literature

Four main Dravidian languages: Tamil, Telugu, Kannada, Malayalam, developed own scripts and literature.

Tamil Literature üìú (Using a scroll to denote ancient nature of Tamil lit)

Antiquity: Oldest of these, literature belongs to early centuries of the Christian era.
Sangams: Three literary gatherings (Sangams) where sages and poets recited compositions (themes: politics, war, love).
Famous Works:
  • "Ethutokoi" (composition of 8 poems)
  • "Tolkappiyam" (Grammar)
  • "Patthuppattu" (The 10 songs)
  • Thiruvalluvar's 'Kural' (verse on life and religion)
  • 'Shilppadikaram' and 'Manimekhalai' (most famous early Tamil works)

D. Medieval Literature üè∞

Early Medieval - North India

Language of literature was initially Sanskrit.

Kashmir Works (Sanskrit):
  • Somdeva's 'Kathasaritsagar'
  • Kalhana's 'Rajtarangini' (first historical books of India)
Other Sanskrit Work: Jaydeva's 'Geetgovind' (finest poems).

Apabhramsa languages started developing into modern Indian languages.

Early Hindi: 'Prithviraj Raso' by Chandbardai (heroic deeds of Prithviraj Chauhan).

Early Medieval - South India

Sanskrit literature made great strides.

Kannada Literature (Jainism influence)

Poet Pampa: 'Adipuran', 'Vikramarjun vijayant'.
Poet Ponna: 'Shantipuran' (life of 16th Jain tirthankar).
Poet Ranna: 'Ajitnathpuran', 'Gada Yudh'.

Pampa, Ponna, Ranna are known as the trio of early Kannada literature.

Tamil Literature

Poet Kamban: Composed 'Ramayana' in Tamil.

Delhi Sultanate Period

Promotion of Indian languages and composition.

Delhi Language Forms: Khadi Boli and Brij Bhasha used for writing. Devotional songs composed.
Rajasthani Language: Heroic poems and stories (e.g., 'Alha', 'Udal', 'Visaldev Raso').
Awadhi Language: 'Chandrayan' by Mulla-Daood (oldest book in Awadhi).

Critical appreciation of ancient books still written in Sanskrit.

Persian Language üìú (Court Language)

Many Persian words seen in Indian languages. Historians emerged.

Ziauddin Barani:
  • 'Tarikh-e-Firoz Shahi' (account of Khilji and Tughluq kings)
  • 'Fatwa-e-Jahangiri' (political theory)

Amir Khusro ‚úíÔ∏è

Outstanding literary figure: poet, historian, mystic saint, music composer. Disciple of Nizamuddin Auliya.

Works: 'Ashiqua', 'Nah', 'Sipiher', 'Kiratul sadayan', 'Khazain-ul-Futuh', bilingual quatrains (Hindi/Persian).
Views: Praised India as 'Earthly Paradise'. Believed Hinduism resembled Islam in essence. Considered 'Hindawli' (Hindi around Delhi) his mother tongue.

Regional Languages & Bhakti Saints üôè

Regional kings gave impetus. Bhakti saints preached in people's languages.

Kabir: Great poet, works in 'Sadhukhadi', Dohas part of folklore.
Malik Muhammad Jayasi: 'Padmawat' in Awadhi.
Tulsidas: 'Ramcharit Manas' in Awadhi.

Literature in Other Languages:

Bengali: Ramayana by Kritta Vasa, lyrics by Chandidas. Saint Chaitanya (devotional songs).
Gujarati: Narsinh Mehta (devotional songs).
Marathi: Namdev, Saint Eknath.
Kashmiri (under Zainulabidin): Mahabharata, Rajtarangini translated into Persian.

Vijaynagar Empire üëë

Sanskrit literature continued. Important period for Telugu literature growth.

Raja Krishnadevarai: Great king, Telugu and Sanskrit writer. Wrote 'Amukta Malayd'.

Mughal Period Literature üïå

Great development in literature, similar to art and architecture.

Royal Authors & Works:

Babar: Pioneer of Turkish poetry. 'Tuzuk-e-Babar' (autobiography in Turkish, later 'Babarnama' in Persian).
Gulbadan Begum: 'Humayun-nama'.
Jahangir: 'Tuzuk-e-Jahangiri' (autobiography).
Aurangzeb: Prolific writer.
Bahadur Shah Zafar: Notable Urdu poet.

Hindi Literature (Akbar's Reign):

Poets: Tulsidas, Surdas.
Keshavdas: Wrote on love and separation.
Rahim: 'Dohas' still popular.

Noteworthy Books & Translations:

Abdul Fazl: 'Ain-e-Akbari' (details Indian customs, manners, religion, philosophy, economy), 'Akbarnama'. (Perhaps unparalleled historical work).
Faizi (Abul Fazl's brother): Great Persian poet, translated Sanskrit works.
Akbar's Translation Department: Translated Mahabharata, Ramayana, Atharvaveda, Bhagwad Gita, Panchtantra from Sanskrit into Persian.

Birth of Urdu Language üó£Ô∏è

Significant medieval development. Became one of richest modern Indian languages.

Great Poets: Wali, Mir Dard, Mir Taqi Mir, Nazir Akbarabadi, Abdullahkhan, Galib, Iqbal.

Urdu prose developed early 18th century (translations from Sanskrit, original works).

Muhammad Hussain Azad: 'Darbar-e-Akbari' (one of best Urdu prose works).

E. The Ancient Universities of India üéì

Ancient Seats of Learning:
These universities were beacons of knowledge, attracting students globally.
Nalanda (Bihar) | Takshashila (Pakistan) | Varanasi (UP) | Vallabhi (Gujarat)

(1) Nalanda University

Location: Badgaon village, Patna district, Bihar.
Traditions: Importance in Buddhist and Jain traditions (Mahavir Swami performed 14 chaturmas here).
Establishment: Kumar Gupta built a monastery here (5th century CE). Fame increased thereafter.
Resources: Thousands of priceless manuscripts. Pious place of Indian culture.
International Students: Students from various parts of the world. Great traveller Heun Tsang (Xuanzang) visited and studied here.
Reputation (5th-11th Century CE): Best education, best libraries. Students studied here and Takshashila. Heun Tsang took 657 books to China.
Infrastructure (7th Century, per Heun Tsang): 7 huge halls, 300 lecture rooms, special monasteries for students.
Sustenance: Villages donated for university upkeep. Free food and clothing from village earnings.
Library: Book repository area known as "Dharmgunj".

A student passing out from Nalanda was considered an ideal student of India.

(2) Takshashila University

Location: Rawalpindi, present Pakistan. Capital city of ancient Gandhar region.
Curriculum: Imparted education in 64 subjects.
Learning System: Most students stayed in ashram with guru. 'Jivak' (disciple of Lord Buddha) learnt Ayurveda here.
Naming Belief: Named after Taksha (son of Bharat, brother of Lord Ram).
Reputation (7th Century BCE onwards, flourished for centuries): Renowned education centre. Students free to choose subjects. Limit of 20 students/teacher (often exceeded).
Student Body: Students from distant cities (Varanasi, Rajgruha, Mithila, Ujjain). Princes of Kashi & Kausal, Panini (grammarian), Kautilya (politician), Chanakya (mentor of Chandragupta Maurya) studied here.
Specialization: Best centre for higher education. Subjects: Vedas, military science, Gajvidhya (elephant lore), archery, grammar, philosophy, warlore, astronomy, astrology.
Visitor: Chinese scholar Fa-Hien visited in early 5th century CE.

(3) Varanasi (Kashi) University

Fame: Place of pilgrimage (7th century CE), famous education centre.
Cultural Hub: Grew as religious centre for Aryan culture in Upanishadic period.
Royal Patronage: King Ajatshatru of Varanasi (great philosopher, supporter of education).
Spiritual Significance: Maharshi Vedvyas had his ashram here (Vyaas Samhita). Lord Buddha chose Varanasi for propagating his teachings.
Philosophical Centre: Adishankaracharya adopted new Vedanta principles here. Vallabhacharyaji & Chaitanya Mahaprabhu (Pushtimarg pioneers) gained prestige for Vaishnav sects.
Migration of Scholars: Majority of scholar families from Punjab migrated to Kashi.
Buddhist Centre: Monastery at Sarnath became famous education centre under Emperor Ashoka's patronage.

(4) Vallabhi University

Location: Gujarat. Famous education centre (7th century CE).
Patronage: Significant contribution from contemporary rulers of Vansh Dynasty and its people.
Religious Focus: Centre of Hinayana cult of Buddhist religion.
Principals (Mid-7th C): Buddhist scholars Sthirmati & Gunmati.
Student Reach: Students from distant Ganga-Yamuna areas. Chinese traveller It-sing (I-tsing) noted Vallabhi competed with Nalanda.
Status: Capital and international port (480 AD - 775 AD).
Maitrak Dynasty: Rulers patronized education (though not Buddhist, they helped the institution).
Decline: In 775 AD, Arabians attacked, Maitraks defeated, university closed down.
Recognition of Scholars: Names of famous scholars written on the gate. Scholars showing erudition got higher rights in Rajsabha (royal assembly).

Vallabhi was an international university in real sense, teaching almost all branches of knowledge. Maintained by charity. World-renowned for arts and science education (Vidhya).
