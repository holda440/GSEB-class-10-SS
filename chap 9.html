B. Deforestation ü™ì
üî∏ Meaning and Impact
  • Meaning: Deforestation is the destruction of forests, a global problem resulting from human progress.
  • Impacts:
    • Increased CO‚ÇÇ in the atmosphere, intensifying the greenhouse effect.
    • Soil erosion and decreased farm fertility.
    • Loss of habitat for countless living organisms.
    • Wild animals move towards urban areas, leading to human-wildlife conflict.
üî∏ Remedies for Forest Conservation üõ°Ô∏è
  • Research & Alternatives: Find alternatives to wood (e.g., solar energy, natural gas) to reduce usage.
  • Afforestation: Compel industries using forest raw materials to practice afforestation. Plant new trees of the same species where cutting is unavoidable.
  • Ban & Control: Implement a total ban on cutting immature trees. Strictly control activities like eco-tourism to prevent damage.
  • Awareness & Education: Organize widespread public awareness programs. Include forest conservation in the school and college syllabus.
  • Community Forestry: Develop Social and Agro-forestry to meet fodder and firewood needs.
  • Fire Management: Create a national-level force or system for dousing forest fires.
  • Pollution Control: Ensure proper clearance of litter left by pilgrims and tourists in forested areas.
  • Pasture Management: Demarcate and reserve specific areas for animal pasture to prevent overgrazing.
C. Wildlife Diversity and Conservation üêº
üî∏ India's Rich Wildlife Diversity
  • India's diverse climate and physiography support a vast variety of insects, birds, animals, and vegetation.
  • Global Rank: India stands 12th in the world in terms of bio-diversity.
  • Species Count: Home to about 81,251 species of animals and birds.
  • Unique Fauna: Includes animals from Asia, Europe, and Africa. Notable species are the black bear, one-horned rhino, snow leopard, Red Panda, tiger, and lion.
  • Migratory Birds: Places like Keoladev National Park (Rajasthan) and Nal Sarovar (Gujarat) host migratory birds in winter.
üî∏ Reasons for the Destruction of Wildlife

üö® Key Threats to Wildlife

  • Habitat Loss: Human interference in grasslands and forests destroys natural habitats.
  • Hunting & Poaching: Hunting for skin, bones, horns, or for hobbies and bio-medicines.
  • Over-exploitation: Human greed leads to over-exploitation of forests for roads, mining, and new settlements, displacing animals.
  • Forest Fires: Engulf many species and destroy eggs and young ones.
üî∏ Wildlife Protection Schemes üêò

India has launched several projects to protect endangered species:

Project Year Started Objective
Tiger Project üêÖ 1971 To save tigers from extinction due to illegal hunting and deforestation. Implemented in about 44 regions.
Elephant Project üêò 1992 To protect elephants, their habitats, and migratory corridors. Implemented in 26 protection zones.
Rhino Project ü¶è Ongoing To protect the One-Horned Rhino, mostly found in Assam. 'Rhino Vision 2020' aimed to increase their number to 3000.
Crocodile Project üêä 1970s To save freshwater alligators from extinction.
Vulture Project ü¶Ö 2004 Started due to an unusual decrease in the population of 9 sub-species of vultures.
Snow Leopard Project üêÜ 2000 To increase knowledge and awareness among local people to protect the snow leopard found at high altitudes.
D. Sanctuaries, National Parks, and Bio-reserves

These three terms refer to protected areas but have key differences in their level of protection and objectives.

Feature Sanctuary National Park Bio-reserve Zone
Main Focus Protection of a specific species. Protection of an entire ecosystem. Protection of physical and cultural diversity of a region.
Human Activity Permitted within certain limits (e.g., grazing with permission). Complete ban on grazing and most human activities. External human movements are totally restricted. Local communities are often part of the system.
Protection Level Protected. More protected. Most protected, formed as per international norms.
Area Size Varies. Varies, but usually larger than a sanctuary. Very large, often over 5000 sq. km.
Examples Perriyar, Eturnagaram Kaziranga, Corbett, Gir Nilgiri, Sundarban, Rann of Kachchh
E. One-Mark Facts for Quick Review ‚ö°
  • Virgin vegetation in India is found in the Himalayas, Sundervan, and Thar desert.
  • India is home to ~81,251 animal species and ranks 12th in global bio-diversity.
  • The Tiger is now extinct from Gujarat's forests.
  • The Cheetah is totally extinct from the forests of India.
  • The Red Panda is found in the cold forests of the eastern Himalayas.
  • The Ganga River Dolphin (Susu) is an endangered freshwater species.
  • Tiger Project was started in 1971.
  • Elephant Project was started in 1992.
  • The Rann of Kachchh in Gujarat was declared a Bio-reserve in 2008.
  • Henotaro (Indian Vulture) is found in arid regions of Gujarat.
  • Dugong is an aquatic animal found along the western sea coast of India.
  • India has 18 Bio-protection Zones, 103 National Parks, and 531 Sanctuaries.
